Project Overview

The Challenge

NFI Industries needed to replace a collection of outdated transportation tools with a unified, scalable TMS platform. Existing processes were heavily reliant on legacy systems and manual workarounds. The complexity of operations, spanning dispatch, tracking, exception handling, and reporting, required a solution that could centralize data while remaining intuitive for both new and experienced users.

  • Replace multiple disparate legacy systems
  • Support complex multi-modal workflows across freight, intermodal, and final mile operations
  • Establish clearer visibility into critical operational data
  • Design for scalability as the user base and business needs evolve

Project Goals

I worked closely with developers, business stakeholders, and product managers to create a platform that balanced operational power with usability. Our north star was to streamline brokers workflows, enable faster onboarding, and improve how teams interact with shipment data day-to-day.

  • Design workflows that align with real user behavior and expectations
  • Build reusable components to enforce visual and functional consistency
  • Create a flexible UI foundation for future feature expansion
  • Support faster iteration and feedback cycles with developers

Development Approach

This wasn't a traditional redesign, we built the TMS platform from the ground up. Our goal was to create a durable, scalable foundation that could evolve alongside the business. I focused on balancing thoughtful UX patterns with a modern component architecture that allowed for rapid iteration, consistent interfaces, and close alignment with backend logic in Phoenix and Elixir.

Design Principles

Component-First Thinking

Built a modular system from day one, enabling reuse, maintainability, and clarity in both design and code.

Performance by Default

Optimized for fast rendering and minimal DOM re-renders with LiveView. Prioritized responsive performance across large datasets.

Progressive Disclosure

Surfaced only what users needed, when they needed it. Prevented visual overwhelm through layered interface logic.

Collaboration-Ready

Wrote components and documentation with other developers in mind, enabling faster onboarding and easier handoff between teams.

Research & Discovery

Understanding the Logistics Landscape

Discovery for this project was ongoing, often embedded directly into our collaborative work sessions, event storms, and site visits. While we didn't follow a formal research playbook, we consistently gathered insights from internal users and stakeholders, shaping the product through conversation, observation, and shared understanding of the logistics domain.

Event Storming as a Shared Tool

Event storming was used throughout the project as a collaborative way to understand workflows, align across teams, and identify opportunities for design and system improvements. My first session was in Cherry Hill, NJ on my second day at NFI, where I worked with developers, product owners, and the department manager to map out key processes. These workshops became a regular part of our discovery process, especially when starting new initiatives or working through domain-specific problems.

User Interviews Across Teams

Over time, I spoke with dozens of users across roles including account managers, operations staff, and regional brokers. Some sessions were one-on-one interviews while others happened during design discussions or feature reviews. Recurring pain points included the rigidity of legacy systems and the amount of workarounds users relied on to complete routine tasks. That feedback helped push us toward more flexible, context-aware interfaces.

"We have workarounds for everything. If something doesn‚Äôt work, we‚Äôve figured out how to get around it by now."

Discovery as a Long-Term Practice

This wasn‚Äôt a one-time research phase. As the product evolved, so did our understanding of how it needed to work. In early designs, we separated work objects across screens to isolate responsibilities. But over time, feedback showed users needed to see and act on everything within a single, unified view. That led to major structural changes in how we organized interfaces, moving toward consolidated screens with shared context and actions.

Artifacts & Visual Thinking

Visual thinking played a big role throughout the project. We used wall-sized sticky note boards, Kanban tracking, and physical mapping to stay aligned. These methods helped surface friction points, connect technical and business goals, and ground our design conversations in real workflows.

Design Process

My design process at NFI was grounded in understanding ‚Äî of the users, the business goals, and the technical context. Whether we were tackling a new initiative or iterating on a live feature, I worked closely with product, engineering, and users to surface the root problem and build toward a solution that was feasible, scalable, and usable.

1. Collaborative Discovery & Ideation

Design work typically began with either a kickoff session or an event storm, depending on the scope. These meetings gave us shared context, helping the team align on what the problem was and how it affected user workflows. From there, we often ran ‚Äúcrazy eights‚Äù sketching exercises to quickly generate multiple ideas on paper. It was an effective way to exhaust assumptions early and shift the team into visual thinking before we touched tools like Figma or Miro.

2. Wireframes & Explorations

We used a mix of Sketch (early on) and Figma (eventually) for wireframing and prototyping. Most features went through several visual variations before we voted on a direction to explore further. One of the most memorable explorations was our time entry project. What seemed simple at first turned into a complex challenge, requiring multiple iterations to account for business rules, workflows, and backend constraints.

3. Iteration Through Feedback

Feedback played a central role in shaping design direction. Product managers were our primary channel to user feedback, but we also received input directly from users and engineering leads. Regular design reviews allowed teammates to ask questions and point out missing use cases. Often, the act of visualizing a flow revealed gaps we hadn‚Äôt considered in discussion.

4. Component System Development

Our design system evolved alongside the product. Early on, it was a fast-moving collection of styles and patterns. Over time, we refined it into a more stable set of components. I often started with Figma variants, but depending on the context, some patterns moved straight from paper sketches to code. We eventually built out more than 50 components, many of which were reused across the platform to ensure consistency and reduce implementation time.

5. Working Side-by-Side with Developers

I worked closely with the development team on implementation, reviewing pull requests and occasionally building components directly in LiveView. The level of my involvement depended on the complexity of the feature. Being part of the build process gave me insight into how backend logic and data structures influenced design, which helped prevent unrealistic handoffs. When needed, we discussed constraints or alternatives together before committing to a design.

The Solution

Our final solution addressed user frustrations around fragmentation, redundancy, and outdated interfaces. We designed a system that prioritized visibility, responsiveness, and task continuity‚Äîempowering dispatchers and managers to work faster, with fewer clicks and clearer information. Below are key areas where the interface and workflow design had measurable impact.

1. Unified Dashboard

The dashboard brought shipment data, load statuses, exceptions, and recent activity into a single view. Instead of navigating between multiple tabs and tools, users could now access the information they needed at a glance‚Äîwith priority-based filters and status indicators helping them focus on what mattered most.

2. Consolidated Shipment View

Shipment details, actions, documents, and messaging were redesigned to live in one place. Previously, users had to switch between systems or screens to complete a task. With the new layout, they could manage everything about a shipment‚Äîplanning, editing, tracking, and resolving‚Äîfrom one continuous interface.

3. Smarter Exception Handling

Exceptions were previously tracked manually, often across emails or spreadsheets. We implemented a categorized exception panel with built-in logic and resolution tools. Users could now identify, assign, and resolve issues quickly without losing context or relying on offline notes.

4. Planning and Time Entry Tools

One of the more complex areas was time entry. What seemed like a simple interaction turned out to require nuanced rules and conditions across departments. We created guided input flows that accounted for regional differences, validation rules, and exceptions‚Äîreducing user errors and saving time.

Key UI Shifts

  • ‚Ä¢ Shifted from tab-based navigation to task-based views
  • ‚Ä¢ Replaced redundant fields with smart defaults and pre-fill
  • ‚Ä¢ Embedded status logic, so users didn't have to guess what came next

Team Collaboration Improvements

  • ‚Ä¢ Enabled shared visibility into loads and exception handling
  • ‚Ä¢ Integrated in-line comments and shared task indicators
  • ‚Ä¢ Reduced back-and-forth across departments

Result

These changes laid the foundation for a faster, more coordinated user experience. Teams could do more from fewer screens, with greater confidence in the system's accuracy and responsiveness.

Reflections & Lessons Learned

Working on the TMS platform wasn‚Äôt just a long-term project ‚Äî it was a long-term education. This experience challenged me to grow as a designer, collaborator, and developer. It gave me the opportunity to shift from supporting workflows to shaping them, and from executing UI to helping architect the system behind it.

What Worked Well

  • Ongoing User Engagement

    Spending time with users in the field helped surface practical, high-impact changes we might have missed in formal requirements.

  • Event Storming for Shared Understanding

    These collaborative sessions became a consistent tool for aligning teams on goals, workflows, and assumptions early in the process.

  • Component-First Design Thinking

    Building a living component system made the platform more maintainable and easier to scale across different use cases.

Challenges & Takeaways

  • Designing for Complexity

    Many logistics workflows aren't linear. Balancing simplicity and power required iteration, progressive disclosure, and a lot of internal feedback.

  • Documentation Gaps

    In the early days, some design decisions were tribal knowledge. Over time I learned the importance of documenting not just what we built, but why.

  • Balancing Design & Dev Work

    As both a designer and LiveView developer, I had to constantly balance fidelity with feasibility ‚Äî and I learned how much clarity helps both sides move faster.

Key Takeaways

This project highlighted several principles that I now apply to all of my UX work:

Context is Everything

Every team, tool, and workflow has a reason behind it. Design that honors that context is not only more usable ‚Äî it‚Äôs more likely to be adopted and improved upon.

Design as a System

A good UI solves problems. A good design system prevents them. Thinking in components early helped our team scale fast without losing consistency.

Iteration Over Assumption

The best ideas often emerged after the third or fourth version. I‚Äôve learned not to get too attached to my first solution, and to leave room for surprises in the process.
